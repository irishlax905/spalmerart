Wagon Ruts into Baker City (Original)

Painted from life during a walk with his dog by the artist, Stephen Palmer ventured to the outskirts of Baker City to learn more about the fascinating history of the Oregon Trail and to gather more reference photos for his upcoming, Old Wood Series. Pioneers of all ages walked a majority of the 2,170 mile journey, through sage brush, dry prairies, over tall mountain passes and deep forests just to get to fertile soil and more opportunity for their families. Around areas like the region depicted in the painting, overlooking Baker Valley and where once The Lone Pine stood, a prominant standing pine tree signaling newcomers nearby. In this area of the Oregon Trail, the land started to flatten out, and allowed for pioneers to spread out from their single file lines. The one of three visible wagon ruts depicted in the painting are some of the only 300 existing miles remaining of visible trail ruts. Other ruts have been eroded or washed away from natural cycles. Miles and miles of sage brush cover the land as it leads into the dusty, cowboy town of Baker City. For more Baker City history, check out this article on the Haunted Geiser Grand Hotel.
